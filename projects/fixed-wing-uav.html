As mechanics-team coordinator, I led sizing and structural decisions, organized sponsor support for foam cutting and 3D printing, and contributed hands-on to fabrication—especially cutting carbon-fiber sheets for load-bearing components and integrating electronics inside the foam core.

What I Did

I coordinated the mechanics sub-team and set up the modeling workflow in Excel and XFLR5 to size the wing, estimate lift/drag, and check stability margins. Beyond analysis and documentation, I sourced and secured sponsor support for CNC foam cutting (fuselage/wing cores) and 3D printing of fixtures and internal mounts. On the shop floor I took part in manufacturing—cutting carbon-fiber sheets for spars and linkages, finishing parts, and fitting electronics inside the foam structure to validate clearances and weight balance.

Modeling & Sizing (Excel + XFLR5)

Our early iterations used Excel to sweep wing loading and power loading, and XFLR5 to compare airfoils, lift curve slope, and stability derivatives. This let us lock the planform and structural requirements before fabrication, and it guided the placement of carbon reinforcement and internal mounts.

Manufacturing & Sponsorships

Cutting foam using a 2-axis CNC wire cutting machine

Carbon-fiber cutting processes using in-house equipment

Integration & Assembly

I worked on embedding electronics into the foam core and validating harness routes, CG, and serviceability. This also fed back into XFLR5 mass-distribution checks and the decision on carbon reinforcement placement.

Lessons Learned

  • Foam is not the best material for load-bearing structures.
  • Sponsorships for foam cutting and 3D printing can compress lead times dramatically.
  • Cutting CF in-house is hard and requires specialized equipment.
